Product Improvements for HarmonyForge
Based on the behavioral observations and direct feedback from the participants, the following technical and experiential iterations should be prioritized:
1. Resolve UI Interaction Bugs and Hotkey Misfires. Participants encountered functional barriers that interrupted their creative workflows. One user noted that when attempting to click and drag a specific note, the system incorrectly grabbed the pitch directly below it. Another participant experienced a consistent glitch where utilizing keyboard arrows to drop a note an octave continuously moved the pitch upward instead of downward. Both users emphasized that their willingness to pay for a subscription is directly contingent upon the stabilization of these UI performance issues.
2. Enhance Auditory Grounding and Timbre Fidelity. The system's auditory playback engine requires refinement to ensure users trust the generated output. A user noted that the playback for selected string instruments (like double bass and viola) sounded "terrible" and "crunchy," prompting them to switch back to a default concert piano sound to accurately evaluate the harmony. High-fidelity playback is essential for "aurally" verifying theoretical suggestions. Furthermore, you should implement a feature that allows users to easily preview how the harmonies will sound across different instrumental configurations before they fully commit to an edit.
3. Transition the Theory Inspector from a Passive to a Proactive Tool. While the inclusion of the conversational Theory Inspector was received positively, it is currently underutilized. One user perceived it merely as a background "search AI" rather than an active assistant that could help them as they worked. To increase its utility, the core logic should be updated to proactively suggest actionable musical alternatives—such as passing chords, alternative voicings, or inversions (e.g., substituting a G major with a G7 or G sus)—which would help musicians overcome creative roadblocks while retaining their unique sound.
4. Isolate Melodic Phrasing from Harmonic Generation Users demonstrated a clear desire to delegate certain tasks to the AI while strictly retaining control over others. One participant explicitly stated they would delegate the finding of chords and harmonizing notes to the AI, but reserved control over the tempo, note length, and overarching musical phrasing or "cadence" strictly for themselves. The interface should visually and functionally separate these parameters, ensuring the AI does not overwrite the human's rhythmic intent when generating vertical harmonies.
